    Literature review

    The literature review is one of the most important, but often the most dread boring part of any research. I know, I've been there too.

    In this thread, I will show you some techniques to make this process easier, and maybe even a little fun.

    üëáüßµ {current}/{total}

    ‚ö†Ô∏è Disclaimer: this advice is based on personal experience from me and my close colleagues. I work in Computer Science, specifically in machine learning.

    Every field is different, but I believe this advice is useful for anyone at least in quantitative fields.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    A literature review can serve many purposes:

    üíÅ An informative review (or survey) to describe a field or area of study

    üîç A critical review to highlight something missing

    ‚öóÔ∏è A supporting review (or related work) to complement an ongoing research

    üëá {current}/{total}

    üíÅ An informative review summarizes the key ideas, methods, and results, of a given area or problem.

    You'll focus on the general trends, the patterns behind what seems to work, and the interesting connections between them.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    üîç A critical survey analyzes a specific issue, like an important overlooked question, or a common methodological error.

    You'll focus on the effects that this issue produces and how it can be traced across the relevant literature.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    ‚öóÔ∏è A supporting review contextualizes ongoing research in a broader body of work.

    You'll focus on the place your results fit in, how are they similar, and how are they novel to existing research.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    Whatever the purpose of your literature review, it is insufficient to just cite chronologically every relevant work.

    ‚≠ê You'll need to find some structure that links and organizes the relevant material so that some general insights can be extracted.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    Here's a process that works for me:

    1Ô∏è‚É£ Make a database of relevant papers 2Ô∏è‚É£ Identify trends 3Ô∏è‚É£ Make a structured outline 4Ô∏è‚É£ Support with graphics 5Ô∏è‚É£ Finish with key highlights

    It all starts with finding relevant papers.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    ‚ùì How to find papers to read?

    I always start with a small seed of papers that I know belong to the general topic. You can build this seed by asking experts: your supervisor, colleagues who work in the area, latest publications in relevant journals...

    üëá {current}/{total}

    You want to build an implicit graph of relevant papers, navigating through their literature reviews, to discover those nodes that everyone seems to cite for a given idea.

    Tools like and make this exploration easier.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    ‚ùì How to know what is relevant?

    What is considered SOTA or relevant changes for every field, but you should in general keep a healthy balance of new versus and seminal works.

    When comparing newer to older papers, weigh their relative relevance by their age.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    Citations can be a meaningful proxy for relevance, but don't rely only on quantitative metrics. Be critical and conscious when assessing relevance.

    A paper with zero citations might be highly relevant for your specific issue.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    1Ô∏è‚É£ Make a database of every paper you read.

    It can be as simple as an excel sheet. For each paper, annotate its main features according to what matters for your review.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    Features can be things like "uses technique X", or "evaluates with method Y". I prefer boolean or categorical features as opposed to free-form comments.

    Read just as much as needed to characterize each paper, and bookmark what you want to read in-depth later.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    You don't need to decide the characteristics up-front. It is rarely possible.

    As you read more papers, you'll discover that some things get mentioned over and over.

    Refactor and expand your list of features as needed, keeping old entries in sync.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    2Ô∏è‚É£ Identify trends and clusters of similar ideas

    Once you have a significant amount of papers annotated, look at the characteristics, and identify the clusters of things that are almost always used together.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    This will give you a map of the key trends, the overarching ideas that almost everyone fits in.

    You'll also quickly discover outliers, papers doing weird things, and missing spots where no one seems to be looking at.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    For example, you might discover that technique X has never been evaluated with method Y, in the specific problem Z.

    If the characteristics you annotated are meaningful for describing the domain you're analyzing, these insights are what you're looking for.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    3Ô∏è‚É£ Make a structured outline

    With the trends and clusters you detected, you can create a taxonomy of the relevant methods and ideas.

    As you create the outline, cross-reference every item with the corresponding citations from the database you already made.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    Make sure your outline focuses on the important bits, according to that what type of literature review you're doing.

    üëâ If it's an informative review, the general trends are more important.

    üëâ If it's a critical review, the outliers might be more meaningful.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    At this point, you're ready to write down the text of the review. Here, all the practical advice about technical writing also applies.

    Be clear and concise. Be critical but in an objective way, praising or criticizing ideas instead of people.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    4Ô∏è‚É£ Support with graphics

    You will often be able to add a table or graph almost directly extracted from your annotations.

    For example, you can select a key subset of features and works, and build a table that summarizes it.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    Some typical examples are:

    üîπ A qualitative comparison between existing approaches, when each is applicable, and their advantages and disadvantages.

    üîπ A graphical representation of the relations between ideas and methods: which is based on, or used for what.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    üîπ A quantitative comparison of experimental results, according to whatever metrics are relevant. This might require you to perform new experiments, so it might be overly complicated.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    5Ô∏è‚É£ Finish with key highlights

    Remember to stress the key points that you wanted to make with your literature review. According to the type of review, you'll focus on different things.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    üîë If it's a survey, summarize the key trends, and highlight the remaining open questions.

    You can also identify the low-hanging fruits interesting to pursue in near-term research and contrast them with long-term issues.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    üîë If it's a critical review, focus on what you discovered: the main blocker that is hampering progress or the overlooked issue that everyone's missing.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    If it's a support review, you want to stress two ideas:

    üî∏ there is something left to be done, a clear spot where your research fits;

    üî∏ and you're approach is reasonable, the existing evidence doesn't rule it out.

    üëá {current}/{total}

    Although literature reviews are probably the most hated part of technical writing for almost everyone, they are a critical element of the scientific method.

    These hints, I hope, will make it easier next time it's your turn to write one.
